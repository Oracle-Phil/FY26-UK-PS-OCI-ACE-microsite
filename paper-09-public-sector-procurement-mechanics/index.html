The Mechanics of Public Sector Procurement

How UK Buyers Choose, Evaluate, and Assure Cloud Infrastructure

A web-native field guide explaining how public sector cloud decisions move through approval gates, procurement routes, evaluation criteria, assurance checkpoints, and board-level scrutiny.

Key takeaway

OCI proposals advance when the architecture is supported by the right evidence pack, aligned to the buyer’s route to market, and framed in the language of risk, assurance, reversibility, resilience, and governance.

Executive orientation

Procurement is not administrative background. It is the mechanism through which UK public sector cloud choices are justified, scrutinised, defended, and eventually converted into contracts.

Why it matters

Cloud designs are tested against decision gates, route constraints, evaluation criteria, and assurance obligations before they can become live services.

Who should read it

OCI architects, account teams, bid contributors, and technical leaders preparing for UK public sector engagements where cloud infrastructure needs to withstand formal scrutiny.

What it helps with

Preparing evidence packs, anticipating approval boards, selecting the right language for each procurement route, and aligning technical design to sector-specific assurance.

What not to overclaim

Do not imply that technical capability alone wins public sector work, that one framework fits every sector, or that Oracle can pre-approve a customer’s assurance decision.

Field interpretation: the practical question is not only whether OCI can meet the requirement. It is whether the customer can evidence, defend, and approve the choice within their own governance model.

How UK public bodies decide

UK public sector procurement is deliberately protracted. It moves through a chain of decision gates designed to test need, value, compliance, and risk before an award is signed. The same broad pattern applies across central government, the NHS, policing, and defence, but each sector layers in its own oversight.

Central government

Spend controls and programme boards

Departments and arm’s-length bodies must clear Cabinet Office Digital and Technology Spend Controls for significant cloud projects. Outline cases are expected to show alignment with the Technology Code of Practice and Government Functional Standard GovS 005.

  • Review by programme boards, design authorities, or architecture authorities.
  • Final approvals usually rest with the SRO, advised by CIO/CDO, SIRO, CISO, Finance Director, and Commercial Lead.
NHS

Information governance and clinical safety

NHS England, Integrated Care Boards, and NHS Trusts shape assurance around information governance, DSPT alignment, DPIAs, and clinical safety where systems affect care pathways.

  • Patient data commonly triggers DPIA and DSPT evidence requirements.
  • Sign-off may involve Trust Boards, ICB committees, SIROs, Caldicott Guardians, and clinical safety officers.
Policing

Local autonomy under national influence

Police forces buy independently but operate under national strategy and assurance influence from the Police Digital Service and the National Policing Digital Strategy 2025 to 2030.

  • National boards review business cases and live services.
  • Local approvals commonly involve CIOs, Force Information Assurance Leads, Commercial Leads, and PASF where hosting or interconnection is relevant.
Defence

Classification-specific accreditation

The Ministry of Defence operates under the Procurement Act 2023 but applies further accreditation through Defence Digital, the Cloud Strategic Roadmap for Defence, and the Defence Digital Foundry.

  • Decision-making flows through programme boards, Defence Commercial, security accreditors, SROs, and senior finance officers.
  • Defence primes may act as intermediaries and carry responsibility for alignment evidence.

Decision gates in cloud procurement

The source paper’s page 3 diagram has been rebuilt as an accessible HTML flow. It shows procurement as a sequence of gates, not a single purchasing event.

  1. Business needDefine the service, policy driver, operational requirement, or transformation outcome.
  2. Outline business caseDescribe value, cost, route options, risk, delivery, and strategic alignment.
  3. Departmental or sector assuranceSpend controls, DSPT, PASF, Defence accreditation, or equivalent gates.
  4. Programme Board / Design AuthorityTest technical approach, governance, delivery model, and assurance readiness.
  5. Procurement route selectedChoose G-Cloud, Cloud Compute 2, Find a Tender, PDS, NHS, defence route, or prime-led path.
  6. Evaluation and assessmentScore against quality, price, social value, resilience, support, and portability.
  7. Final approvalsSRO, CFO, Commercial Lead, security, finance, and senior accountable officers.
  8. Award and contract assuranceComplete contract checks, mobilisation conditions, KPI publication, and go-live readiness.
Architectural implication: each gate asks for a different kind of evidence. A strong architecture diagram is important, but it will not substitute for DPIA support, NCSC principle mapping, exit commitments, cost control evidence, and sector artefacts.

Default routes to market

Public sector buyers in the UK do not simply choose cloud providers directly. They use approved commercial routes that define how requirements are expressed, how competition is conducted, and how awards are documented.

Route explanations

G-Cloud 14: the most widely used catalogue for cloud hosting, software, and support. Buyers can direct award from catalogue listings or run a further competition, with call-offs constrained by published service definitions.

Cloud Compute 2 (RM6292): used for larger infrastructure and platform services, especially where requirements exceed catalogue call-offs or require multi-year, outcome-based contracts.

Procurement Act 2023 procedures: used where frameworks do not apply. The Competitive Flexible Procedure is common for complex data-centre exits or multi-tower transitions, while the Open Procedure suits well-specified requirements.

NHS, PDS, and defence routes: sector-specific routes overlay central rules with DSPT, PASF, Defence Digital, accreditation packs, national boards, or prime-led models.

Evaluation criteria that decide outcomes

UK public sector procurements are commonly assessed through a blend of quality, price, and social value. The balance varies by route and complexity, but the source paper highlights recurring patterns that ACEs should prepare for.

Quality versus price splits

Central government procurements must apply the Social Value Model, with at least 10 percent of the total score allocated to social value. A typical pattern is 60 percent quality, 30 percent price, and 10 percent social value.

Technical merit and assurance

Quality is often measured through technical merit, compliance with the Technology Code of Practice, and alignment to NCSC cloud security principles.

Predictability of cost and delivery

Evaluators look for evidence of tagging, budgeting, showback or chargeback, anomaly detection, and consumption monitoring. Confidence that budgets will not be destabilised matters more than isolated price-performance claims.

Portability, resilience, and support

Exit planning, switching support, disaster recovery design, RTO/RPO commitments, incident management, escalation, and ongoing reporting are all common scoring concerns.

Assurance and compliance checkpoints

Procurement decisions are not final when an award notice is issued. Major contracts must still pass assurance and compliance checks before services go live. The common sequence is data protection, security, and sector-specific assurance.

Checkpoint domains

Data protection and privacy: most cloud procurements require a DPIA. Buyers expect suppliers to support this with data flow diagrams, storage locations, and retention practices.

Security: the NCSC Cloud Security Principles remain the baseline assurance framework. Security teams assess data protection in transit, identity and access control, audit logging, incident response, and the shared responsibility model.

NHS: DSPT compliance is mandatory, and clinical safety artefacts such as hazard logs and safety cases may be required when systems affect clinical decisions.

Policing: PASF remains relevant where cloud services interconnect with facilities or require specific secure handling.

Defence: cloud services at OFFICIAL or above must map to NCSC principles and pass Defence accreditation regimes.

Portability and switching: authorities increasingly ask for termination, exit, switching, and data portability evidence up front.

Checklist for assurance readiness

  • Prepare a DPIA support pack covering data flows, storage, retention, and processing locations.
  • Map services against the 14 NCSC Cloud Security Principles.
  • Document the shared responsibility model, logging, and incident response.
  • For NHS bids, supply DSPT evidence, information governance alignment, and clinical safety artefacts if required.
  • For police bids, confirm PASF requirements and provide accreditation evidence.
  • For defence bids, assemble a security case aligned to Defence Digital accreditation.
  • Include exit and portability commitments in the bid pack.

Recent awards, patterns, and lessons

Public buyers leave a trail. Award notices set out the route chosen, the criteria applied, and the justification given. Read collectively, they show how cloud is actually being bought during 2024 and 2025.

Defined and familiar services

G-Cloud 14 continues to dominate hosting and hosting-adjacent awards. Departments, NHS bodies, police forces, and even the MoD use it through direct awards or mini-competitions, commonly within the familiar 3+1 structure.

Where scope is narrow, evaluation can follow suit. Price-only decisions may look like commoditisation, but often reflect how tightly the service has been defined.

Complex and strategic transitions

Complex transitions are increasingly channelled through the Competitive Flexible Procedure. The source paper cites HMRC’s data-centre exit as a visible example, with staged governance running to 2028.

The critical distinction is governance: risk logs, migration controls, accreditation milestones, and performance measures that can be published in assessment summaries.

The rhythm to absorb: catalogue awards for the defined and familiar, flexible procedures for the complex and strategic, and desktop assessments as connective tissue between the two.

Implications for OCI architects

Procurement mechanics decide whether an OCI proposal advances, stalls, or is ruled ineligible. Every design must be backed by an evidence pack aligned to decision gates, evaluation criteria, and assurance requirements.

Practice 01

Build the evidence early

Bids rarely succeed on technical diagrams alone. DPIAs, NCSC-aligned mappings, portability statements, and cost-predictability notes are faster to tailor when a baseline set already exists.

Practice 02

Select the route with the customer

A G-Cloud call-off constrains evaluation to service descriptions, while a staged procedure under the Procurement Act 2023 allows dialogue and iteration. NHS and policing add overlays such as DSPT or PASF.

Practice 03

Anticipate the boards

Spend controls, programme boards, and assurance committees test risk mitigation, reversibility, resilience, and governance. Architects who use this language help sponsors defend OCI under scrutiny.

Practice 04

Keep the artefact alive

Frameworks evolve, weighting shifts, and assurance expectations rise. Awareness must remain current, just as the designs themselves must remain current.

Practical field use

This paper is best used when an engagement needs to become procurement-aware, not just technically persuasive. It should inform discovery questions, bid preparation, evidence packs, and customer workshops where assurance, value, and risk are being shaped.

Discovery questions to ask

  • Which route to market is being considered?
  • Which boards or assurance bodies will review the design?
  • Which sector artefacts are mandatory?
  • Which criteria will be scored, and what evidence is expected?

Evidence to prepare

  • NCSC Cloud Security Principles mapping.
  • DPIA support pack.
  • Shared responsibility summary.
  • Cost predictability and FinOps notes.
  • Exit and portability statement.

Language to use

  • Risk mitigation.
  • Reversibility.
  • Resilience and tested DR.
  • Governance and accountability.
  • Board-defensible evidence.

Language to avoid

  • Price-first positioning without full lifecycle evidence.
  • Implied pre-approval from assurance bodies.
  • Generic public sector claims that ignore sector overlays.
  • Technical superiority claims without route-specific relevance.

Conclusion

Procurement is where strategy meets reality. It is the process through which public sector bodies turn ambition into contracts, and where OCI must demonstrate not only technical capability but also compliance, portability, and accountability.

The source paper traces how decisions are made, which routes to market are available, what criteria tip the balance, and where assurance is tested. Its value is not in memorising every notice, but in understanding the structure and parallels across sectors.

The knowledge should be treated as a living artefact. Frameworks evolve, weighting shifts, and assurance expectations rise. Keeping awareness current is as important as refining the designs themselves.
