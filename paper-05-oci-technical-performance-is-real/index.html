Executive orientation

Read this before using the performance story
Why it matters

Performance claims need evidence

Public-sector platform choices must withstand procurement scrutiny. This paper exists because customers are rightly sceptical of broad cloud performance claims.

Who should read it

OCI ACEs and customer-facing architects

It is most useful for colleagues preparing for technical customer conversations where benchmark credibility, workload fit, latency or cost-performance will be challenged.

What it helps with

Connecting architecture to outcomes

The source traces the role of off-box virtualisation, flat RDMA-enabled fabric, flexible bare-metal shapes and autonomous database optimisation in reducing latency or improving throughput.

Do not overclaim

Not a universal win

The source is explicit that another platform may still be the better fit. Use OCI performance as a workload-specific argument, not a claim that OCI wins everywhere.

Field principle: start from the workload outcome, then map to measurable targets, then introduce the OCI architectural capability that could help.

Benchmark caution

Why cloud benchmarks struggle to convince

Cloud providers frequently advertise superior performance, but experienced architects know that benchmarks can be shaped by instance selection, test settings, sponsorship bias and undisclosed assumptions. The source uses that scepticism as the starting point rather than trying to dismiss it.

Technical reality adds further complexity. Cloud performance varies with hardware revisions, noisy-neighbour effects and placement within a region. Averages can look healthy while tail-latency spikes still break service-level objectives.

Credibility test

Can the result be repeated?

The source points to open, peer-reviewed suites such as MLPerf, alongside SPEC, TPC and FinOps Foundation style approaches, because disclosure and reproducibility matter more than isolated victories.

Credibility test

Does it match the workload?

Customers increasingly judge platforms by transparent results aligned to their own workload patterns. A benchmark win is useful only when the tested pattern resembles the production reality.

Architecture stack

The design foundations behind OCI performance

Flat Clos fabricNon-blocking topology and predictable hop count
End-to-end RDMARoCE v2 for HPC, GPU and storage paths

  1. Bare-metal first compute

    OCI exposes the whole processor to a tenant, including cores, caches and NUMA topology, removing hypervisor overhead for latency-sensitive workloads.

  2. Off-box network and storage virtualisation

    A custom SmartNIC executes virtual switching, overlay tunnelling, firewall rules and NVMe-oF target code. Moving these paths off the host CPU frees guest cores and reduces noisy-neighbour interference.

  3. Flat, non-blocking Clos fabric

    The source describes a single-tier spine-leaf network without oversubscription, with production superclusters measuring around 2 microseconds hop-to-hop latency and 400 to 800 Gbps of usable bandwidth per node.

  4. End-to-end RDMA transport

    The same RoCE v2 fabric powers HPC messaging and remote block storage. Block Volume LUNs over NVMe-oF are cited at 1.3 million IOPS per VM with sub-100 microsecond service times.

  5. NVMe-everywhere flash storage

    Bare-metal and many VM shapes include local NVMe SSD, while Block Volume is built from the same media and protocol pattern, supporting linear throughput scaling with volume size.

Production relevance

Where the architecture delivers in practice

Customer example

AI and HPC training at scale

National Grid ESO is cited as using OCI GPU superclusters for power-forecasting models, improving accuracy by 40 percent and reducing model-run time from hours to minutes compared with its previous environment.

Internal reference

Predictable bare-metal performance

An internal, NDA-bound UK investment-analytics reference is cited as eliminating the 15 to 20 percent performance variability seen on other clouds by moving financial-risk simulations to OCI bare-metal AMD servers.

Caveats and objections

Limitations and misconceptions to preserve

The source deliberately keeps caveats visible. That restraint is part of the credibility of the paper and should be retained in customer-facing preparation.

Objection

"OCI only wins in contrived benchmarks."

Handle by pointing to reproducible suites such as MLPerf and SPEC, then move quickly to named production outcomes such as National Grid ESO and NHS Shared Business Services.

Objection

"No enterprise really needs that level of performance."

Reframe the discussion around public-sector workloads where latency, jitter, batch windows or forecasting cycles connect directly to policy compliance, service outcomes or operational stability.

Objection

"Performance is irrelevant if features lag."

Acknowledge that OCI's catalogue is narrower than AWS or Azure, while noting that the source says the gap has narrowed in core IaaS and data services.

Handling posture

Be candid before being persuasive

The strongest use of this paper is not a defensive rebuttal. It is a balanced account of when OCI design choices matter and when ecosystem, region or tooling needs may point elsewhere.

Field guidance

How to discuss OCI performance without hype
  1. Lead with outcomes, not components

    Ask what the workload must achieve, such as millisecond response, hour-ahead forecasting accuracy or predictable month-end close. Link the need to latency, throughput or time-to-train before introducing an OCI feature.

  2. Use diagnostic questions to uncover constraints

    Establish variability tolerance, analytic cycle length, dominant cost element and regulatory limits on data movement or residency.

  3. Position OCI through reproducible evidence

    Reference independent benchmarks and named customer results rather than Oracle-authored metrics. Offer a scoped proof-of-concept so the customer can validate results under its own governance controls.

  4. Provide balanced comparisons

    Acknowledge competitor strengths, then explain how OCI addresses the specific performance gap. The tone should respect AWS, Azure and Google while clarifying where OCI behaves differently.

Ask

Diagnostic questions

  • What variability in response time is acceptable before an SLA breach?
  • How long does a full analytic cycle or model-training run last today?
  • Which cost element dominates the budget, compute, storage or data egress?
  • Are there regulatory limits on where data may transit or reside?
Say

Balanced comparison language

"AWS offers more functions in this category; OCI provides lower tail latency for tightly coupled clusters."

This kind of phrasing preserves competitor respect while making OCI's differentiator specific and testable.

ACE summary

Key takeaways for OCI ACE
Architecture

Know the mechanism

Understand how off-box virtualisation and RDMA fabric remove host-CPU bottlenecks and reduce jitter for AI, HPC and Exadata workloads.

Evidence

Use external proof

Position price-performance advantage by referencing MLPerf, SPEC, TPC and independent cost studies rather than relying on authored benchmarks.

Outcomes

Move beyond lab tests

Use examples such as NHS Shared Business Services and National Grid ESO to connect architectural design to production impact.

Integrity

Acknowledge limits first

Surface ecosystem size, regional coverage and tooling gaps before the customer does, then guide towards multi-cloud or partner patterns when they are the better answer.

Proof

Offer a scoped PoC

Use Terraform scripts and open monitoring so stakeholders can verify performance under their governance controls.

Economics

Connect to budget oversight

Link lower egress pricing and OCI-Azure adjacency to the financial discipline common in UK public-sector projects.

Conclusion

Performance by design, proven in the field

The paper concludes that OCI price-performance should be treated as an engineering outcome, not a marketing promise. SmartNIC-based off-box virtualisation, non-blocking RDMA fabric, bare-metal tenancy and an all-NVMe storage tier are presented as design choices that reduce hidden performance taxes such as hypervisor cycles, network oversubscription and spindle latency.

The strongest closing message for ACEs is not that every workload belongs on OCI. It is that reproducible data, transparent proof-of-concept work and an honest acknowledgement of cases where another cloud may fit better can help public-sector organisations select infrastructure with trust, integrity and defensible evidence.
