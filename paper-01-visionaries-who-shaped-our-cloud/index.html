Paper 01 | Cloud history and competition

The Visionaries Who Shaped Our Cloud

Learning from the Pioneers Who Defined the Modern Infrastructure Landscape

This first paper places OCI inside the wider history of cloud infrastructure. It traces the people and decisions behind public cloud, virtualisation, warehouse-scale computing, cloud-native software, Microsoft’s cloud transformation and Oracle’s second-generation cloud reimagination.

Key takeaway

OCI’s present architecture should be understood as part of a broader lineage: technical progress comes from deliberate design decisions, not market momentum alone.

Executive orientation

Why this paper matters

The paper opens the series by establishing context. It treats modern cloud infrastructure as the result of repeated acts of imagination, engineering discipline and commercial courage. For UK Public Sector ACEs, that history is useful because it explains why today’s customer expectations exist, and why OCI’s architecture was deliberately shaped as an answer to earlier cloud constraints.

Who should read it

ACE teams who need a concise story of how cloud evolved, why competitors deserve respect, and how OCI fits into the wider infrastructure landscape.

What it helps with

Opening strategic conversations, explaining OCI’s design intent, and grounding Oracle’s cloud narrative in industry history rather than isolated product claims.

What to preserve

The balanced respect for AWS, VMware, Google, Microsoft, IBM, Docker, Kubernetes, Terraform and Oracle pioneers. The paper is not a competitor-dismissal piece.

What not to overclaim

Do not imply Oracle alone shaped modern cloud. The source argument is that many pioneers set expectations, and OCI’s choices should be understood in that context.

Source-derived article section

Introduction

Cloud infrastructure did not simply appear. It was imagined, engineered and evangelised by people who looked beyond the constraints of their time and chose to reshape how technology is consumed.

Elasticity, virtualisation, scale, resilience and portability each began as ideas that many considered impossible or impractical. Understanding the people behind those ideas helps us see why the competitive landscape looks the way it does today.

The paper deliberately asks us to respect competitors and pioneers alike. Their contributions set standards that still inform the work Oracle does and the expectations customers bring to cloud conversations.

Paper-specific pattern

Infrastructure lineage

This timeline preserves the source paper’s narrative sequence and turns it into a field-readable map of how major cloud ideas entered the market.

1

The early vision: the birth of cloud

When Amazon Web Services launched in 2006, many saw rented infrastructure over the internet as a limited experiment. Andy Jassy, Werner Vogels and James Hamilton recognised a deeper need: rapid scale, lower risk and pay-as-you-go consumption.

Jassy guided AWS from an internal project into a platform that redefined how organisations build and run software. Vogels applied distributed systems principles such as designing for failure. Hamilton contributed data centre power, cooling and network design that helped hyperscale economics work.

2

Virtualisation and hybrid thinking

Before public cloud could take hold, the industry had to share computing resources securely and efficiently. VMware pioneers Diane Greene and Pat Gelsinger helped make virtual machines the essential building block for modern infrastructure.

At IBM, Arvind Krishna pushed hybrid thinking further through the Red Hat acquisition, placing open-source platforms such as Kubernetes at the centre of enterprise modernisation.

VMwareHybrid cloudOpen source
3

Engineering for scale: the Google approach

Google’s cloud impact began with its internal need to run enormous services reliably and cost-effectively. Urs Hölzle treated infrastructure as a single unified system, with warehouse-scale computing replacing traditional hardware silos.

Google pioneered software-defined networking, automated resource management and cluster orchestration through systems such as Borg. Thomas Kurian later shifted Google Cloud towards enterprise adoption, industry solutions, multi-cloud and more predictable customer engagement.

Article section

The cloud-native revolution

While hyperscalers built global infrastructure, a new wave of innovators reimagined how software should run on it. Solomon Hykes created Docker, popularising lightweight containers that made applications easier to deploy, scale and move across environments.

Google engineers Joe Beda, Brendan Burns and Craig McLuckie recognised that thousands of containers needed a new control plane. Kubernetes, released as open source in 2014, became the standard for orchestrating containerised workloads.

Mitchell Hashimoto added another foundation with Terraform. By making infrastructure as code accessible and practical, Terraform allowed teams to treat cloud resources like software: version-controlled, repeatable and transparent.

Field lesson 4

Lasting impact often starts with smaller teams who share practical ideas. Open collaboration can set new standards faster than any single company could achieve alone.

Article section

Microsoft’s transformation

Satya Nadella became CEO of Microsoft in 2014 and repositioned the company around a mobile-first, cloud-first principle. That shift meant embracing open source, supporting Linux and investing heavily in Azure as a developer and enterprise platform.

Mark Russinovich helped guide Azure’s architecture for scale, security and reliability, including support for confidential computing, live-site debugging and broad Linux compatibility. Scott Guthrie brought a developer-first mindset through services such as Azure App Service, Cosmos DB and Visual Studio integration.

The source paper’s point is cultural as much as technical: Azure’s rise required Microsoft to challenge its own legacy thinking and build a more open, collaborative operating model.

Field lesson 5

Cultural shifts can be as transformative as technical breakthroughs. Openness and a willingness to challenge legacy thinking have reshaped entire organisations.

Article section

Oracle’s reimagination

Oracle’s second-generation cloud reflected conviction and urgency. Larry Ellison judged that extending Oracle’s existing enterprise software portfolio would not be enough to compete with hyperscalers. Instead, he prioritised a clean-sheet architecture centred on security boundaries, predictable performance and dedicated infrastructure for critical workloads.

Ellison appointed Don Johnson, who had previously helped grow AWS, to establish an engineering organisation capable of designing OCI from the ground up. Johnson assembled teams with experience from across the industry and set the mandate to deliver a distinctly enterprise-focused proposition.

Clay Magouyrk translated that vision into foundational architecture, including non-blocking networks, off-box virtualisation and bare-metal compute models optimised for demanding workloads such as HPC and large-scale AI training.

As OCI expanded, Karan Batta shaped product direction for HPC and AI infrastructure, including GPU instances, Supercluster architecture and AI-optimised compute. Mahesh Thiagarajan drove engineering execution, scaling OCI globally while introducing advanced networking and storage designs. Chris Gandolfo helped bring these capabilities to market, working with customers to demonstrate OCI’s strengths in AI and high-performance workloads.

Oracle’s approach to distributed cloud and multi-cloud also reflects the source paper’s theme of customer choice. Dedicated Region, Alloy and partnerships with other hyperscalers signal that providers can align around customer needs without compromising capability.

Field lesson 6

Clear priorities and focused engineering made OCI possible when many doubted it could be done. The same discipline will be needed as the platform adapts to new demands.

Practical field interpretation

Reflections for our mission

The paper identifies repeated qualities across the pioneers: curiosity, willingness to test constraints, technical courage, and focus on customer benefit. Many breakthroughs began as unpopular opinions: virtual machines, pay-as-you-go compute, containers and infrastructure as code.

Technical elegance alone is not enough. AWS, Azure, Kubernetes and OCI each mattered because they removed friction and helped customers focus on their own goals.

For OCI teams, this history creates perspective. OCI’s dedicated isolation, predictable performance, distributed cloud and multi-cloud approach did not emerge by accident. They reflect deliberate choices about where Oracle would differentiate and which customer needs earlier cloud generations did not fully address.

Conclusion

Purpose grounded in experience

The competitive landscape was shaped by people willing to reimagine what technology could become. Clear technical priorities, combined with persistence, can redefine what is possible and set expectations that others must follow.

Looking back at these milestones helps explain why some ideas succeed. It also reminds us that progress depends on detail, questioning assumptions and making deliberate decisions.

As OCI work continues, this history helps Oracle teams see their own contributions in context and approach what comes next with purpose grounded in experience rather than sentiment.

Profiles of the pioneers

The appendix is rebuilt here as accessible profile cards. Use the filters to focus on a domain without losing access to the full set.

Andy Jassy

AWS

As the founding leader of Amazon Web Services, Andy Jassy took AWS from a small internal experiment to the world’s largest cloud platform. His conviction that infrastructure could be sold as a utility service changed how businesses consume technology and helped create the global cloud market.

Werner Vogels

AWS

Amazon’s CTO established principles of scalable and resilient distributed systems. His philosophy that “everything fails” drove designs that anticipate faults rather than only reacting to them.

James Hamilton

AWS

James Hamilton led innovations in power, cooling and network design for hyperscale data centres, contributing to the efficiency and performance that underpin modern cloud economics.

Urs Hölzle

Google

Google’s first Senior Vice President of Technical Infrastructure built warehouse-scale computing into reality. His teams pioneered software-defined networking and automated resource management.

Larry Ellison

Oracle

Oracle co-founder Larry Ellison championed OCI by insisting on secure isolation, predictable performance and autonomous services, enabling a differentiated alternative to first-generation cloud providers.

Don Johnson

Oracle

Recruited from AWS, Don Johnson led the engineering effort behind Oracle’s Gen 2 Cloud, building the teams and designs that helped turn Oracle into a credible hyperscale competitor.

Clay Magouyrk

Oracle

One of OCI’s founding engineering leaders, Clay Magouyrk guided architecture from early design through global expansion, with focus on performance, security and large-scale AI infrastructure.

Karan Batta

Oracle

Karan Batta led product management for OCI high-performance computing and AI services, guiding GPU instances, Supercluster architecture and AI-optimised compute offerings.

Mahesh Thiagarajan

Oracle

Mahesh Thiagarajan oversaw OCI’s global expansion and delivery of advanced networking, storage and compute capabilities central to large-scale distributed training.

Chris Gandolfo

Oracle

Chris Gandolfo helped bring OCI’s AI infrastructure to market and worked with enterprise and AI-focused organisations to demonstrate Oracle’s performance, scalability and efficiency.

Thomas Kurian

Google

After two decades at Oracle, Thomas Kurian became CEO of Google Cloud in 2019 and shifted the platform towards enterprise adoption, multi-cloud capabilities and industry-specific solutions.

Satya Nadella

Microsoft

As Microsoft CEO, Satya Nadella introduced a cloud-first strategy that transformed the company’s culture and made Azure a core part of Microsoft’s identity.

Mark Russinovich

Microsoft

Azure CTO Mark Russinovich brought deep operating systems expertise to building secure and scalable cloud, influencing live-site debugging, confidential computing and Linux support.

Scott Guthrie

Microsoft

Scott Guthrie, EVP of Cloud and AI at Microsoft, co-founded .NET and oversaw Azure engineering, with a focus on developer experience and approachability.

Pat Gelsinger

VMware

As VMware CEO, Pat Gelsinger promoted hybrid cloud as the enterprise default, with initiatives such as VMware Cloud on AWS helping bridge on-premises and cloud environments.

Diane Greene

VMware / Google

Diane Greene co-founded VMware and led the commercialisation of x86 virtualisation. Later, she served as CEO of Google Cloud, bringing an enterprise perspective.

Arvind Krishna

IBM

IBM CEO Arvind Krishna architected the Red Hat acquisition that reshaped IBM’s hybrid cloud strategy around open-source platforms and Kubernetes.

Solomon Hykes

Docker

Founder of Docker, Solomon Hykes helped popularise containers as a standard way to package and deploy applications across environments.

Joe Beda, Brendan Burns and Craig McLuckie

Kubernetes

This group created Kubernetes at Google and released it as open source. Kubernetes became the de facto standard for orchestrating container workloads.

Mitchell Hashimoto

Terraform

As HashiCorp co-founder, Mitchell Hashimoto created Terraform and related tools that made infrastructure as code accessible and practical across clouds.

Adrian Cockcroft

Netflix / AWS

Adrian Cockcroft pioneered microservices, chaos engineering and open-sourced tools such as Simian Army, influencing best practices for resilient scalable systems.
